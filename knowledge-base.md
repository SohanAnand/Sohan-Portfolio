### Trip Booking Concierge | Autonomous AI Agent
**Live at https://trip-booking-ai-agent.vercel.app/**

**What it is:**
An autonomous AI agent that books flights and hotels end-to-end, with a two-step approval gate so it never charges your card without asking first. Built as a 0-to-1 demonstration of trust-first agentic product design.

**Why it was built:**
Autonomous travel agents fail when they book the wrong flight. The failure mode is not a UX bug — it is a $2K charge reversal nightmare. Sohan defined the 0→1 vision for a trip-booking agent specifically engineered to eliminate those irreversible errors. The core insight: most autonomous agents optimize for speed. This one optimizes for trust. The friction of a two-step approval gate is not a limitation — it is the product.

**Tools & Features:**
- Multi-step planning loop with tool-calling for flight search, hotel availability, and itinerary assembly
- Two-step approval gate that surfaces every irreversible action — bookings, payments, schedule changes — for explicit user confirmation before execution
- Structured outputs and fact provenance wired into every agent response so users can trace every claim back to its source
- Adversarial red-team test coverage across 12+ scenarios

**Key Technical Decisions:**
- Chose a two-step approval gate over silent execution deliberately. The prevailing pattern in autonomous agents (full autonomy) breaks the moment a hallucination meets a credit card. The gate converts a trust problem into a UX interaction.
- Wired fact provenance into every response so users are never asked to trust a claim they cannot verify. This was a product decision, not an engineering constraint.
- Deployed as a live demo where reviewers can actively try to break it. The test coverage is the proof of concept.

**Results:**
- Zero approval bypasses across 12+ adversarial red-team scenarios
- 100% fact provenance on every agent response
- Unsafe-booking risk cut to zero
- Live and testable at https://trip-booking-ai-agent.vercel.app/

**Why it matters:**
Trip Booking Concierge demonstrates Sohan's approach to agentic AI product design: identifying the failure mode that matters most (irreversible financial errors), designing the system architecture around preventing that failure (the trust-by-default approval gate), and validating the design against adversarial scenarios rather than happy paths. The medium is the message: shipping a live agent that reviewers can try to break is itself the product thinking.

**Tools & Features:**
- Claude API (language model powering grounded, context-aware responses)
- Supabase (backend and persistence layer)
- pgvector (vector store for semantic retrieval over the knowledge base)
- TypeScript and React (frontend chat interface)
- Real-time response streaming
- Fallback handling for graceful degradation

**How it works:**
Sohan designed the RAG pipeline end to end. He chunked and embedded a structured knowledge base of his career, projects, and product philosophy into pgvector on Supabase, then wired retrieval into Claude's API so every response is grounded in real source material rather than hallucinated. The result is a persistent chat interface deployed on the portfolio with real-time streaming and fallback handling for reliability.

**Key Technical Decisions:**
- Chose RAG over a fine-tuned model deliberately. The knowledge base evolves as Sohan ships new work, and retrieval keeps answers accurate and up-to-date without the cost and rigidity of retraining.
- Used pgvector on Supabase rather than a standalone vector database to keep the stack lean, the latency low, and the deployment simple.
- Built streaming responses into the chat UX so the interaction feels conversational rather than transactional.
- Designed fallback handling so that retrieval failures or API hiccups degrade gracefully instead of breaking the experience.

**Why it matters:**

The problem was not missing features — it was that users were leaving before they could experience the features that existed. That reframe changed everything: instead of building more, we fixed the path to value, and shipped 4 targeted interventions that closed the renewal.

**Q: Have you ever made a wrong call and how did you handle it?**

I build tight feedback loops, measure everything, and treat every decision as a hypothesis to be validated rather than a commitment to be defended. The instrumentation I build is specifically designed to surface wrong calls early so the cost of being wrong stays low.

### AI and Technical Depth

**Q: How do you stay current with AI developments?**
A: I actively track AI research, model releases, framework updates, and emerging capabilities. I do not wait for features to be packaged into products. I read about new techniques and immediately experiment with applying them to real problems. Kira AI, BrandBrief AI, and Trip Booking Concierge are the most recent examples — each one pushed a different frontier of applied AI. My goal is continuous upskilling toward the frontier, and I treat every project as an opportunity to push closer to that frontier.

**Q: What is RAG and how have you used it?**
A: RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. It is a technique that combines a language model with a vector search layer so the model can retrieve relevant information from a knowledge base before generating a response. I have used RAG in multiple shipped projects. In Kira AI — the assistant you are talking to right now — I used Claude API with pgvector on Supabase to ground every response in my actual career knowledge base, so answers are accurate rather than hallucinated. In BrandBrief AI, I used RAG with Claude and pgvector to allow users to describe a design task in natural language and retrieve semantically matched brand templates. At Fabrix, I shipped RAG pipelines with custom chunking strategies and reusable retrieval layers that lifted golden-set eval relevance by approximately 10 percentage points.

**Q: What is fine-tuning and when would you use it instead of RAG?**
A: Fine-tuning adapts a base model's weights by training it on domain-specific examples, embedding the knowledge into the model itself rather than retrieving it at inference time. RAG retrieves relevant context at inference time from an external knowledge base. The trade-off is that fine-tuning is better for well-defined, stable tasks where you want to bake in behavior or style, while RAG is better for evolving knowledge bases where you need up-to-date, traceable answers. At Fabrix, I replaced GPT-4 with a fine-tuned open-source LLM in production for a well-defined IT operations task — eval quality held flat and per-token cost dropped significantly. For Kira AI, I chose RAG because my knowledge base evolves constantly as I ship new work. The right choice always depends on whether the task is stable (fine-tuning) or dynamic (RAG).

**Q: What are eval harnesses and why do you build them?**
A: An eval harness is a systematic framework for measuring whether an AI pipeline is working correctly before and after changes. I use two primary approaches: golden-set evaluation, where you define a fixed set of test cases with known correct answers and measure how often the system produces them; and LLM-as-judge, where a separate language model evaluates the quality of outputs against defined criteria. At Fabrix, I built eval harnesses for every RAG pipeline and the fine-tuned LLM, and ran blind comparisons vs. staff baselines before every rollout. At Community Dreams Foundation, I use the same approach before deploying any AI agent to production. Eval harnesses are not optional engineering overhead — they are how you build confidence that the system will behave correctly in production, and how you prove that quality to non-technical stakeholders.

**Q: What is multi-agent orchestration and when would you use it?**
A: Multi-agent orchestration is a design pattern where multiple AI agents collaborate, each handling a specific part of a complex task, with shared context passed between them. You use it when a problem is too complex or too long for a single model to handle well, or when different parts of a workflow benefit from specialized agent behavior. At Fabrix, I designed multi-agent workflows for IT incident management where one agent detected anomalies, another classified the incident, a third generated remediation recommendations, and the chain had to hold together under real production conditions. In Trip Booking Concierge, I designed a planning loop with specialized tool calls for flight search, hotel availability, and itinerary assembly. The hard part is not building the agents — it is reliability, context management, failure handling, and knowing when to hand back to a human.

**Q: How do you evaluate whether an AI feature is working?**
A: I define evaluation criteria before the feature ships. I use golden-set evaluation to measure accuracy against known-correct outputs, LLM-as-judge to assess output quality against defined criteria, and blind comparisons against human baselines to establish that the AI matches or exceeds the standard it is replacing. For BrandBrief AI, the primary metric is retrieval accuracy (72% on the current prototype) and secondary metrics are time to first match and user adoption. For Trip Booking Concierge, the metric that mattered most was zero approval bypasses across adversarial scenarios — because the failure mode was financial, not informational. AI features are not "done" when they ship. They need continuous measurement and tuning.

**Q: What is the difference between a good prompt and a bad prompt?**
A: A good prompt gives the model the context it needs, constrains the output format, specifies the reasoning approach, and accounts for edge cases. A bad prompt is vague, assumes the model has context it does not have, and produces outputs that require significant manual correction. I understand how language models process context and how token limits affect output quality. I use that understanding to structure prompts that produce reliable, actionable reasoning rather than generic responses.

**Q: What do you think about "agentic AI" — hype or real shift?**
A: Real shift, not hype — but most companies calling their products "agentic" today are shipping chatbots with extra steps. The genuine shift is from systems that answer questions to systems that take multi-step actions against a goal, with their own planning, tool use, and self-correction. The hard part of agentic AI is not building a single agent — it is reliability, context management across steps, failure handling, and knowing when to hand back to a human. Trip Booking Concierge is a concrete example of where this matters: the failure mode of a fully autonomous agent is not a bad answer, it is an irreversible financial transaction. The trust-first design I built around that insight — the two-step approval gate — is what separates a useful agentic product from a liability.

**Q: What is an "AI wrapper" and why do you care about the distinction?**
A: An "AI wrapper" is a product that puts a thin UI on top of an off-the-shelf LLM API without adding proprietary data, workflow depth, or retrieval context. I care about the distinction because wrapper products have almost no defensibility — the moment the underlying model gets cheaper or a competitor ships a similar UI, the business evaporates. The products I build are not wrappers. They ground the LLM in structured knowledge and shape the retrieval and prompting layer around a specific user problem. Defensibility in AI products comes from data, context, and workflow — not from the model.

**Q: What does "invisible AI" mean to you?**
A: Invisible AI means the best AI features are the ones users never think about as "AI." They just experience a friction disappearing — onboarding that used to take six hours now takes 90 minutes, template discovery that used to require scrolling through folders now takes a sentence of natural language, a manual triage step now resolves itself. The moment a product has to label a feature "✨ AI-powered" to justify its existence, something has probably gone wrong upstream in the problem definition. I optimize for the outcome, not the label.

**Q: Walk me through the biggest trade-offs you have made in your career.**
A: A few stand out. First, **RAG vs. fine-tuning** — for Kira AI I chose RAG because the knowledge base evolves; at Fabrix I chose fine-tuning for a well-defined IT ops task because the cost savings were significant and the task was stable. The right choice depends on the problem. Second, **scope vs. speed** at Baseline Tech — I cut a six-month feature plan down to 14 weeks by ruthlessly removing anything that did not directly validate the core hypothesis, and still hit 85% feature completion while shipping seven weeks ahead of schedule. Third, **pipeline vs. platform** at Qliken — I turned down $250K in near-term enterprise pipeline to redirect engineering toward retention fixes, because the cohort data showed the requested features would have less than 5% adoption. That decision recovered $285K in ARR and kept the roadmap defensible. Each trade-off was a choice between an easy "yes" and a harder, data-supported "no in service of a bigger yes."

### Product Philosophy

**Q: What is your product philosophy?**
A: Find the true root cause of the problem, not the surface symptom. Generate multiple solutions and evaluate them against real constraints. Build the best solution, not the easiest one. Measure everything before and after. Make trade-offs explicitly and defend them with data. Ship, iterate, and scale what works. I do not believe in strategy without execution or execution without strategy. They are the same thing done at different time horizons.

**Q: What is your take on the current wave of AI features being shipped everywhere?**
A: Most AI features should not exist. A lot of what gets shipped today is what I call "AI slop" — features bolted onto products to ride the hype cycle without solving a real user problem. Adding AI to everything does not make a product smarter; it makes it noisier. The discipline I try to hold is simple: the right question is never "how do we add AI?" but "what friction is genuinely worth removing, and is AI the right tool to remove it?" The best PMs in this era are the ones brave enough to kill low-value AI features and focus engineering capacity on the ones that actually change user outcomes.

**Q: How do you decide what to build next?**
A: I combine behavioral data, customer discovery, competitive analysis, and business impact modeling to prioritize. The framework I use most often is RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort), but I apply it with full context from cohort analysis, win/loss interviews, and churn data rather than as a mechanical scoring exercise. The most important judgment call is always whether I am solving the right problem before choosing how to solve it.

**Q: How do you balance short-term customer requests with long-term platform investments?**
A: The heuristic I use is: single-customer requests get prioritized only when they address a need that is also present in a broader segment and when building for that customer creates reusable platform capability. If a customer request would create a one-off integration or a hard-coded workaround, it goes to the bottom of the backlog regardless of the account size. At Fabrix, I ensured that no more than 30% of sprint capacity went to single-customer requests while the remaining 70% served the broader product roadmap.

**Q: How do you define and measure product success?**
A: I define success at multiple time horizons. Short-term: activation metrics and time-to-value. Medium-term: 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day retention cohorts. Long-term: expansion revenue, NPS, and feature adoption as a proxy for durable value delivery. I also track the north star metric and the specific input metrics that drive it, ensuring every team member understands how their work connects to the company's growth objectives.

**Q: How do you work with engineering teams?**
A: As a partner, not a requirements dispenser. My computer science background means I can engage with engineering at a technical level, understand scope and feasibility estimates, identify technical risks in the roadmap, and propose phased delivery approaches that allow the team to ship incremental value. I write detailed PRDs with acceptance criteria, edge case documentation, data flow diagrams, and rollback plans so engineering has complete context before sprint commitment. I also protect engineering from scope creep and last-minute priority changes by maintaining a clear, data-driven backlog that the team can trust.

**Q: Tell me about a time you had to lead without authority.**

At Community Dreams Foundation, I aligned 3 stakeholder groups on an AI transformation roadmap where none had prior experience with LLM systems. Authority is slower than trust. I build trust by being right about the data, following through on commitments, and making every stakeholder's constraints visible to everyone else.

**Q: How do you handle ambiguity?**
A: I treat ambiguity as a problem to be decomposed. When requirements are unclear, I ask what outcome we are optimizing for and work backwards from there. When data is missing, I identify the smallest experiment that would give us the signal we need. When stakeholders disagree, I find the underlying data question that the disagreement is really about and go get the data. Ambiguity is rarely irreducible. It usually means you have not identified the right question yet.

**Q: Describe your experience with 0-to-1 product building.**

I built Trip Booking Concierge from zero to a live autonomous agent with a trust-first approval gate and adversarial test coverage. I built BrandBrief AI from zero to a working RAG-powered prototype with brand compliance scoring. At Baseline Tech, I took a product from concept to validated MVP with $60K in pilot commitments in 14 weeks as COO. At Fabrix, I shipped RAG pipelines and a fine-tuned LLM in production. At Community Dreams Foundation, I am building and deploying AI agents to replace manual workflows end-to-end. **Q: Tell me about a product decision you got wrong.**
A: At Qliken early in my PM tenure, I over-indexed on a major enterprise request for a customizable dashboard builder. The customer was vocal, the pipeline value looked good, and I pushed to get it on the roadmap in the next sprint. Engineering built it in five weeks. Six weeks after shipping, the feature had under 8% adoption — even among the accounts that had originally requested it. I had confused expressed desire with revealed behavior. After that, I built the cohort-analysis and low-adoption signal framework that later informed the $285K ARR recovery decision where I deprioritized two enterprise-requested features because the data showed less than 5% similar-feature adoption. The lesson from the first miss made the second call possible.

**Q: What is a weakness you are actively working on?**
A: I over-index on quantitative signal. When I have good cohort data, clean funnel metrics, and retention curves, I move fast and confidently. I have learned the hard way that the data is necessary but not sufficient — there is qualitative stakeholder signal, political context, and organizational readiness that numbers do not capture. I now deliberately pair every major data-driven call with at least two or three one-on-one stakeholder conversations before committing. The weakness is not the analytical rigor — that is a strength. The weakness is trusting it so much that I under-invest in the human signal that surrounds it.

**Q: Have you ever disagreed with a leadership decision and lost?**
A: Yes. I do not win every argument, and I do not think I should. When I lose a decision, my rule is simple: disagree hard in the room, commit hard outside the room. I have had roadmap calls go differently than I wanted, feature prioritizations that I argued against get built anyway, and timelines I thought were aggressive approved. In each case, once the decision is made, I shift into making it succeed rather than litigating it in hallways. The credibility that buys you is more valuable than any single call.
